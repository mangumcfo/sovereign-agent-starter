# Strategic Finance For Growth

*The Intersection of Strategy and Finance in the Pursuit of Growth*

**If your team still builds forecasts the slow way, this is for you.** Here is the shift, in plain terms — read the bolded lines and you have the whole argument:

---

*You have been working on this idea for months. Maybe years. You have sketched it on napkins, pitched it to your spouse over dinner, and run the numbers on a spreadsheet that keeps getting more complicated. Now the business is real — you have an LLC, a bank account with your first deposit, and a list of things to do that would fill a notebook. The exhilaration is undeniable. So is the quiet voice asking: am I going to make it? The answer depends on the decisions you make in the next twelve to eighteen months — and most of those decisions are financial.*

The start-up phase of business growth has the highest number of businesses entering and exiting as they bring their new product or service to market. This makes sense — there are far more one-year businesses in the world than hundred-year businesses. The goal for each firm is to reach the growth phase as quickly as possible to achieve a higher probability of long-term survival. Common estimates point to significant turnover of businesses during this time. The natural evolutionary path is not particularly friendly to businesses that lack a competitive product or service, or where customers are not interested in the offering — a lack of market. Newly minted entrepreneurs, or start-up CEOs, set the initial direction of the company including decisions regarding business planning, market entry and channels, product and service scope, financing, and human capital.

You, the new CEO, are focused on dialing in your value proposition, targeting and reaching the most advantageous customers, measuring your competitive advantage in the marketplace, and increasing your sales level. You may reach out to a small business nonprofit or a friends-and-family network to provide general business support including marketing, sales, operations, accounting, and other finance guidance. What you get is what you pay for — but you are determined.

With the help of your support network, you develop a business plan to define your conceptual strategy and address any business or financial risks you might foresee. Much of your time is spent testing and refining your concept of customer needs and the market. You are diligently learning more about them and what will bring them — wallets open — to you. You wear many hats and are becoming broadly knowledgeable in each aspect of your business, yet you are reliant on affordable specialists or tools to run the show.

Through this segment of the business lifecycle, you may be spending more in cash (often through invested capital) than you have coming in through organic sales. This negative cash flow is dangerous financially, but you have developed a business plan where you have identified your cash burn rate and are motivated to move the needle in a positive direction, according to your plan, to reach positive cash flow in short order. This need is especially compounded if you have tapped into your network for initial funding for business operations. Businesses that require a quick return of invested capital are most at risk. Unless a profitable niche is found and tapped quickly, investors will be hesitant to invest additional funds.

Your spending is focused on the absolutely critical requirements of the operation, which might include additional labor to fulfill customer orders but little else. Building a financial nest egg through profitable sales is probably on your mind, and anything that might compromise that is deprioritized. Each business eventually reaches a crossroads: it either breaks even or its options are depleted and it decides to exit.

Your successful business eventually reaches positive cash flow as you pass your profitability breakeven point — your sales increase beyond your expenses. This growth has come at a cost, and you are feeling the strain on your time and bandwidth. You start to look for efficiencies that can free up your time and take over some of the activities that have less impact on your core business. Depending on that need, you might have replaced Aunt Jill's bookkeeping help and brought in a part-time or full-time bookkeeper, an AR or AP clerk, a payroll or inventory control specialist, or the like. You are quickly progressing toward the next stage: Growth.

As the CEO of your startup, it is your responsibility to decide how your organization is going to function on a daily basis and the strategic direction that will ensure its survival. Previous experiences you have had with your business have helped you shape this direction and make the decision to start up your own operation. You continue to shape this direction by looking into your business to identify what aspects are strong or weak and looking externally to what opportunities might be available to help you grow or what risks are present that would prevent your success. You have either formally or informally developed some goals and objectives for the next couple of years and are using the resources you have available — people, relationships, machinery, real estate — to put daily activities in place to achieve those goals. Or you may not have enough resources at your disposal to get started. If you have not already, it is time to put these thoughts on paper and develop your first strategic plan.

One of the first tasks of the startup CEO is to create a written business plan. Your initial business plan should set the strategic direction of your firm. Having a financial advisor to assist with the development of this plan would be beneficial; however, this activity is largely produced by the startup CEO. Although you likely have already created one for your firm, I have included a synopsis of a generic business plan structure with basic elements below. This plan should be adjusted and expanded upon as business needs change, at least annually as part of your strategic planning process.

**Executive Summary:** A brief combined summary of the entire business plan. This summary is usually contained on one page with a few paragraphs highlighting the most important aspects of your plan that the reader will care about, such as vision statement, product or service description, and financial highlights.

**Company Description:** Discuss what makes your company different, the competitive landscape, your leadership and expertise in the market, and what will make you a success.
